An In-Depth Psychological Analysis

The Impact of Artificial Intelligence on College Students

Saurav Kumar & Vidushi Sonkar

Abstract Introduction Methods Results Qualitative Data Discussion Conclusion

Abstract

[cite_start]

The objective of this study was to investigate the psychological impact of AI on undergraduate students, examining whether AI fosters academic and personal growth or acts as a barrier to development[cite: 3]. [cite_start]A mixed-methods approach utilizing methodological triangulation was employed, combining survey questionnaires and focus group discussions for a comprehensive analysis[cite: 5]. [cite_start]A sample of 253 students from diverse disciplines across India were surveyed[cite: 6]. [cite_start]The findings provide empirical insights into AI's dual role in education, informing policy recommendations for responsible AI integration in academic settings[cite: 12].

Introduction

[cite_start]

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is a transformative domain within computer science that aims to simulate human intelligence processes through machines, especially computer systems[cite: 17]. [cite_start]College students now commonly utilize AI-based tools such as ChatGPT, Grammarly, and GitHub Copilot to assist with academic writing, coding, content summarization, and idea generation[cite: 24]. [cite_start]However, this integration presents a spectrum of engagement, from occasional reliance to deep integration in daily academic routines[cite: 27].

This study explores this relationship through the lens of AI as both a facilitator and a barrier, analyzing eight key psychological dimensions:

    [cite_start]
  • Positive Dimensions: Cognitive Enhancement, Productivity, Skill Development, and Psychological Well-being[cite: 51, 53, 55, 57].
    • [cite_start]
  • Negative Dimensions: Cognitive Overreliance, Ethical Concerns, Psychological Stress, and Social Deficits[cite: 60, 63, 65, 67].

Methodology

Design and Sample

[cite_start]

The study followed a mixed-methods design to comprehensively explore the psychological impact of AI[cite: 118]. [cite_start]The sample comprised 253 undergraduate students from universities across India, representing diverse academic disciplines[cite: 120]. [cite_start]Participants were selected through purposive and snowball sampling methods[cite: 123].

Tools and Instruments

Two self-constructed Likert-scale questionnaires were administered:

    [cite_start]
  • AI Usage Questionnaire: A 30-item survey covering six dimensions of AI engagement, including frequency, purpose, and ethical awareness[cite: 135].
    • [cite_start]
  • Psychological Impact Questionnaire: A 32-item survey assessing the eight positive and negative psychological dimensions mentioned earlier[cite: 137].
[cite_start]

Additionally, a Focus Group Discussion (FGD) was conducted with 13 participants to explore nuanced perspectives on AI's academic and emotional impact[cite: 141].

Quantitative Results

[cite_start]

Statistical analysis using one-way and two-way ANOVA rejected the null hypotheses, revealing significant differences across AI user groups[cite: 173]. [cite_start]Students with high AI usage reported significantly greater positive psychological impacts (cognitive enhancement, productivity, etc.) and lower negative impacts (stress, ethical concerns) than moderate, low, and control users[cite: 458, 459]. Qualitative Findings from FGD

Thematic analysis of the Focus Group Discussion provided rich, contextual insights into students' experiences. Below are the key themes that emerged, along with representative quotes.

Subthemes: Academic Applications, Personal Use.

[cite_start]

Verbatim: "Sabse zyada use hota hai...for assignments and presentations." [cite: 229] [cite_start]Another student noted, "Even if unki zindagi mein koi normal life problem bhi hoti hai, they go to ChatGPT." [cite: 229]

Subthemes: Efficiency, Dependency, Memory Loss.

[cite_start]

Verbatim: "Pehle books kholte the, answers dhoondte the...ab seedha ChatGPT." [cite: 230] [cite_start]This convenience came at a cost, as one participant shared, "Hum cheezon ko totally yaad nahi kar pate...memory power bahut zyada kam hai." [cite: 230]

Subthemes: Subjective Nature, Procrastination, Plagiarism.

[cite_start]

Verbatim: "Teacher jab questions banwate hain AI se, toh wo ethical hai...student ke liye nahi?" [cite: 230] [cite_start]This quote highlights the perceived double standard and ethical ambiguity students face[cite: 468].

Subthemes: Career Transformation, Skill Necessity.

Verbatim: Students recognized AI proficiency as a critical career skill. [cite_start]"Jo AI efficiently use kar paye, wahi job layega." [cite: 239] [cite_start]This reflects an understanding that AI literacy is essential for future employability[cite: 477].

Discussion

[cite_start]

The study's findings align with the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM), where perceived usefulness and ease of use increase adoption and satisfaction[cite: 364]. [cite_start]High AI users demonstrated greater confidence and experienced AI as an enhancer, while low-to-moderate users reported more anxiety and dependency[cite: 362, 461]. The qualitative data underscores a significant concern: cognitive offloading. [cite_start]Students admitted to bypassing critical thinking in favor of instant AI solutions, illustrated by quotes like, "Google bhi nahi karte, direct ChatGPT use karte hain"[cite: 392]. [cite_start]This points to a potential erosion of independent problem-solving skills[cite: 62]. [cite_start]Furthermore, a strong need for curriculum reform was voiced, with students advocating for formal AI education to bridge the digital literacy gap, especially for those from rural backgrounds[cite: 472, 471].

Limitations and Challenges

This study has several limitations. [cite_start]First, it relied on self-reported data, which may be subject to social desirability bias[cite: 481]. [cite_start]Second, the sample was limited to Indian universities, which may affect the generalizability of findings to global student populations[cite: 483]. [cite_start]The questionnaires, while pilot-tested, are not yet standardized instruments[cite: 486]. [cite_start]Finally, the qualitative data was drawn from a single focus group, and the rapidly evolving nature of AI means these findings are a snapshot in time[cite: 488, 491].

Conclusion

[cite_start]

The study concludes that while AI can act as a powerful academic and psychological facilitator, its benefits are closely tied to the user's level of familiarity, access, and critical engagement[cite: 503]. [cite_start]Without structured education and ethical clarity, AI risks becoming a tool of dependency rather than empowerment[cite: 504]. [cite_start]Educational institutions must adopt comprehensive AI literacy programs that balance innovation with ethical and psychological safeguards, ensuring students are equipped not only to use AI effectively but also to understand its broader implications[cite: 505].
